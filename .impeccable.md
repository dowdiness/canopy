## Design Context

Canopy's UI direction is **Legible Instrument**: a thinking instrument that helps
people understand structure, inspect change, make consequential decisions, and
recover from mistakes.

This context is grounded in:
- [Legible Instrument design direction](docs/superpowers/specs/2026-07-19-legible-instrument-design.md)
- [Human-centered product principles](docs/architecture/human-centered-product-principles.md)

Future UI work should use this file as the concise design gate, not as a
component API or renderer architecture.

### Product Quality Model

Usability means reducing five costs:

1. **Translation cost** — converting a human goal into product or implementation
   terminology.
2. **Orientation-recovery cost** — finding one's place after the interface
   changes.
3. **Verification cost** — establishing whether a system proposal is correct and
   appropriate.
4. **Action-uncertainty cost** — predicting what an available action will do.
5. **Recovery cost** — returning to a valid state after a mistake.

An improvement must reduce at least one cost without materially increasing
another. Fewer controls, fewer clicks, or greater visual novelty are not enough.

Significant interactions make this causal model legible:

```text
Context → Artifact → Change → Decision → Trace
```

- **Context:** object identity, revision, save state, and the event requiring
  attention.
- **Artifact:** the persistent object being read or edited.
- **Change:** the proposed difference, reason, confidence where meaningful, and
  affected scope.
- **Decision:** preview, edit, apply, reject, and undo.
- **Trace:** authorship, sources, causal revision, and history.

Accessibility is an equivalence invariant: visual, keyboard, and screen-reader
paths expose the same evidence, reasons, decisions, and recovery paths. The
canonical authority, contestability, reversibility, pacing, and data-ownership
invariants live in the human-centered product principles.

### Design Principles

1. **Human authority** — selecting, previewing, or inspecting a proposal does not
   mutate the artifact. Consequential changes remain governed by explicit
   acceptance or a revocable policy chosen by the person.
2. **Cognitive stability** — artifact identity, selection, focus, scroll position,
   and relevant expansion state survive preview and apply transitions whenever
   technically possible.
3. **Legibility and reasons** — proposals show what changes, what remains fixed,
   why the change is proposed, and which facts support it.
4. **Reversibility** — reversible actions expose immediate undo; irreversible
   external effects require explicit approval before execution.
5. **Negotiable structure** — inferred grouping, hierarchy, emphasis, and
   projection remain visibly distinguishable from user-authored organization and
   can be corrected, suppressed, or replaced.

### Compositional Design

Each screen or bounded workspace has **one focal question**. Every region has a
role in answering it: subject, cause, evidence, alternative, action, or result.
A component does not bring its own claim to visual primacy.

Relative emphasis belongs to the composition rather than the component. The same
content may be primary in one context, supporting in another, and reference
material in a third. A workspace has at most one primary region.

Canopy uses six spatial relationships:

- **Stack:** sequential reading or action.
- **Cluster:** short peers with equal weight.
- **Split:** two responsibilities that benefit from simultaneous view.
- **Grid:** multiple objects compared on shared axes.
- **Inset:** supporting information attached to a local subject.
- **Overlay:** a short task that must suspend the underlying workflow.

Spacing communicates semantic distance:

```text
within element < within group < between groups < between responsibilities
```

Related and comparable information shares alignment anchors across component
boundaries when that supports scanning or comparison. Boundaries are economical:
a visible boundary communicates independent identity, responsibility, selection,
movement, or overlap. Parent and child regions do not repeat equivalent framing.

Color is a shared attention budget managed by the composition. Components request
semantic roles; they do not introduce local accent systems. Control placement
communicates target and consequence: local actions stay near the affected object,
workspace actions appear at the end of the decision flow, and preview, apply,
external execution, and undo remain perceptually distinct.

Responsive reflow preserves the focal question, evidence, alternatives, action
mapping, and reading order. It may change simultaneity, density, columns, and
spacing; it does not remove a decision-critical comparison axis. Motion is
coordinated around the causal event: one input produces one dominant response,
and stable content does not replay entrance motion.

Composition laws:

1. **Focal clarity:** one question remains visually primary.
2. **Relationship fidelity:** spacing, alignment, and boundaries reflect semantic
   relationships.
3. **Action locality:** a control's target and consequence are apparent from
   placement and wording.
4. **Information parity:** reflow retains the evidence required for the same
   decision.
5. **Identity stability:** the same artifact remains recognizable across preview,
   update, and projection.
6. **Authority:** selection and preview never imply commit.
7. **Reversibility:** combined reversible operations define a coherent reversal
   boundary.
8. **Accessible order:** visual, keyboard, and assistive-technology order express
   the same relationships.
9. **Negotiability:** inferred structure remains distinguishable and correctable.

### Visual and Interaction Constraints

- **Foundation:** use a light, slightly warm neutral foundation; hierarchy comes
  from order, spacing, alignment, typography, and fine rules.
- **Color roles:** ordinary content stays neutral; interactive and selected states
  use one consistent cool accent; warning is amber; error or destructive is red;
  verified, protected, or complete is green. Color is sparse and redundant with
  text or shape.
- **Typography roles:** UI uses the platform system font by default; code,
  identifiers, and syntax use a legible monospace font. Weight, spacing, and
  position establish hierarchy before size or color.
- **Focus behavior:** keyboard focus appears immediately, remains visible, and
  stays at the causal interaction point after an update unless the task
  explicitly moves to a new object.
- **Touch targets:** interactive targets are at least 44 CSS pixels.
- **Response timing:** press feedback begins on pointer-down or key activation
  and targets 80–100 ms; selection transitions target 120–160 ms; inline
  disclosure targets approximately 200 ms.
- **Reduced motion:** preserve state feedback through color, opacity, or
  immediate change while removing vestibular movement.
- **Preview before apply:** system-originated changes remain previews until
  explicitly applied or covered by a revocable policy.
- **Stable artifact identity:** previews and applies preserve artifact identity,
  selection, focus, scroll, and expansion state whenever technically possible.
- **Revision trace:** accepted changes create traceable revisions with authorship,
  sources, causal revision, and history.
- **Undo:** reversible actions expose immediate undo; undo creates a new traceable
  revision rather than erasing history.

### Validation Gates

Evaluate each significant UI change against observable task outcomes:

- Can a person identify the current object, state, and revision before acting?
- Can they locate the changed information without losing their place?
- Can they predict the result of each available action from its label, placement,
  and surrounding context?
- Does the update preserve location, focus, selection, and relevant expansion
  state whenever technically possible?
- Do desktop, mobile, keyboard, and screen-reader paths retain the same
  decision-critical evidence and actions?
- Can the person inspect, reject, correct, apply, and reverse system proposals?

A polished appearance does not compensate for failure on these measures.
